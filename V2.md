To improve your model’s accuracy, we need to address the "Template Overfitting" problem. Currently, your model is learning specific sentence structures (like "Play [X] and get [Y] at [Z]") rather than the actual _intent_ of the message.

Here is a detailed procedure to improve your agent’s performance.

---

## 1. The Data Procedure (The "80/20" Rule)

Machine Learning is 80% data and 20% code. Your current data is too "perfect" (generated) and repetitive.

### A. Diversify the "Safe" Dataset (Thai Language)

Your safe data is mostly English or very short Thai sentences about weather/food. When you test it against a long Thai spam message, the model sees many Thai characters it has **never seen** in a "Safe" context, so it guesses "Spam" by default.

- **Action:** Add 500+ lines of "Normal" Thai text.
- **Sources:** Use Thai Wikipedia snippets, news headlines (Thairath/Matichon), or generic business emails.
- **Why:** This teaches the model that words like "สมาชิก" (member), "ระบบ" (system), and "ข้อมูล" (data) are used in safe contexts too, not just in gambling.

### B. "Noise" Injection in Spam

Your spam data is too clean. Real-world Thai spam uses "Character Hacking" to bypass filters.

- **Action:** Add samples that look like this:
  - `ส ล็ อ ต` (added spaces)
  - `ส.ล็อต` (added punctuation)
  - `p-g-slot`
- **Why:** This trains your n-gram vectorizer to find the connection between broken characters.

---

## 2. Technical Improvements (Code Logic)

### A. Better Thai Tokenization

Currently, you use `split_whitespace()`. Thai does not use spaces between words. While your bigram/trigram logic helps, it is very "heavy" on memory.

- **Improvement:** Use a **Syllable-based** or **Dictionary-based** tokenizer.
- **Recommendation:** If you want to stay in pure Rust without heavy dependencies, keep your n-grams but increase the range. Use **Trigrams (3)** and **Quadgrams (4)** specifically for Thai.
  - _Example:_ "เครดิตฟรี" (Free credit)
  - Bigram: `เคร`, `รด`, `ดิ`, `ิต`, `ตฟ`, `ฟร`, `รี` (Too small, meaningless)
  - Quadgram: `เครด`, `รดิต`, `ดิตฟ`, `ิตฟร`, `ตฟรี` (Much more unique to spam)

### B. Adjust Laplace Smoothing

In your `BernoulliNB::fit` function, you use `+ 1.0` and `+ 2.0` (Standard Laplace).

```rust
let val: f64 = (feature_counts[i][j] + 1.0) / (class_counts[i] + 2.0);
```

- **Improvement:** If you have a massive vocabulary (due to n-grams), a smoothing of `1.0` might be too strong, making every word seem "possible" in every class. Try a smaller alpha (Lidstone smoothing), like `0.1`.

---

## 3. Feature Engineering

### A. The "Link" Feature

Almost all "Real World" spam includes a URL or a Line ID (e.g., `@123cash`).

- **Procedure:** Don't just rely on the Bayes model. Add a pre-processor:
  ```rust
  fn contains_spam_indicators(text: &str) -> bool {
      text.contains("line.me") || text.contains("@") || text.contains("bit.ly")
  }
  ```

### B. The "Thai vs English" Ratio

Gambling spam often mixes Thai keywords with English brand names (PGSlot, Joker).

- **Procedure:** Create a feature that counts the ratio of English characters to Thai characters. Pure Thai or Pure English is usually "Safe." Mixed (50/50) with gambling keywords is "Spam."
